# Health-Infastructure-Inequality-of-NYC

This project explores the relationship between neighborhood income, transit access, food environments, fitness center density, and obesity rates across New York City. The central hypothesis is that higher-income, more transit-connected neighborhoods disproportionately attract premium health infrastructure, including boutique fitness studios and full-service grocery retailers such as Whole Foods, while lower-income neighborhoods in the outer boroughs are left with limited fitness options and grocery stores that offer fewer healthy choices at higher costs. These structural disparities, added on by uneven subway access that creates transit deserts in outer boroughs, make it harder for residents of lower-income neighborhoods to maintain healthy lifestyles regardless of individual choices. The project will visualize these patterns using Tableau, drawing on publicly available datasets to construct a neighborhood-level picture of health infrastructure inequality across NYC.
